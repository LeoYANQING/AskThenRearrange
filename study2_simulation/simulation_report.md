## 1. Executive Summary
This report presents the results of a fully simulated within-subjects HRI user study (N = 24) comparing three preference-elicitation strategies in PrefQuest: Direct Querying (DQ), User-Preference-First (UPF), and Parallel Action-then-Rule (PAR). The simulation was designed to reflect theoretically motivated effect sizes and plausible individual differences, and serves as a power analysis and instrument validation exercise prior to data collection.
The primary objective measure, unseen PSR, confirmed the hypothesised ordering (UPF: M=0.780 > PAR: M=0.643 > DQ: M=0.565), with Friedman χ²(2) = 22.333, p < .001. The H1 dissociation between UPF and DQ was supported after Holm correction (W = 11.0, p < .001, r = 0.811).
Subjective scales revealed the hypothesised dissociation (H3): PAR received the highest Perceived Understanding (M = 5.014) despite lower unseen PSR than UPF, while UPF produced the highest Interaction Agency (M = 5.396). Overall the simulation suggests the design is sufficiently powered to detect core effects at N = 24, and all three Cronbach's α values for the questionnaire scales exceeded .70.

## 7. Hypothesis Verification
### H1 (UPF unseen PSR > DQ): **SUPPORTED**
UPF M = 0.780 vs DQ M = 0.565; Wilcoxon W = 11.0, p < .001, r = 0.811. Effect size is large (r ≥ .50).

### H2 (DQ ≈ UPF CL > PAR CL): **SUPPORTED**
DQ CL M = 4.833, UPF CL M = 4.438, PAR CL M = 3.243. DQ vs PAR: p < .001 (***); UPF vs PAR: p < .001 (***); DQ vs UPF: p = .059 (ns — as predicted).

### H3 (PSR order ≠ PU order — dissociation): **SUPPORTED**
Unseen PSR order: UPF (0.780) > PAR (0.643) > DQ (0.565). PU order: PAR (5.014) ≥ UPF (4.812) > DQ (3.563). The rankings diverge, confirming the dissociation between objective preference capture and subjective sense of being understood.

## 9. Potential Issues and Recommendations

1. **Learning Order Effects**: Despite Latin Square counterbalancing, the conceptual complexity of UPF (abstract rule elicitation) may be easier to engage with after participants have already experienced PAR or DQ. Recommend adding an order covariate in analysis and checking for strategy × order interactions.

2. **Scene Confound**: Some scenes (e.g., kitchen) may have more culturally agreed organising norms, inflating PSR for all strategies. Recommend randomising scene assignment further, or using mixed models with scene as a random factor.

3. **IA Item IA3 ('I felt I had control over what conclusions the agent drew about my preferences')**: This item targets conclusion-control (did the user shape the agent's inferred rules?). Under DQ the agent draws no explicit conclusions, so control is low (M≈3.6); under UPF the user's stated rule IS the conclusion, giving high control (M≈5.3); under PAR the confirmation step grants moderate control (M≈4.0). This formulation improves IA3 coherence with IA1/IA2 under PAR and resolves the near-zero α issue seen with the old termination-decision framing.

4. **CL Items and PAR**: The PAR confirmation format may lower CL not because it reduces mental demand but because it shifts effort to the agent. CL3 ('annoyed') may capture frustration with DQ repetition rather than cognitive load per se. Consider separating frustration from effort items.

5. **Sample Size for Three-Way Post-Hoc**: With N=24 and Holm correction for 3 pairs, the UPF vs PAR comparison on unseen PSR may be underpowered at α=0.05. Recommend pre-specifying UPF vs DQ as the primary test, and treating the full ordering as exploratory. Simulation suggests UPF vs DQ is robustly detectable; PAR vs DQ is detectable but at lower power.

6. **Interview Structure**: The 5-theme template ensures coverage but may lead to experimenter-led responses. Use open-ended probes first and only follow up with direct comparisons if not spontaneously addressed.

## 10. Formal Hypotheses (Recommended)

Based on simulation findings, the following formal hypotheses are recommended for the paper:

**H1 (Objective Preference Capture):** UPF will yield significantly higher unseen PSR than DQ (primary pre-registered hypothesis; Wilcoxon signed-rank, Holm-corrected α = .05, one-tailed directional; expected r ≥ .50 based on simulation).

**H2 (Cognitive Load):** PAR will yield significantly lower CL than both DQ and UPF (two pre-specified Wilcoxon tests, Holm-corrected); DQ vs UPF CL difference is exploratory (no directional prediction).

**H3 (Perception–Performance Dissociation):** PAR will yield higher PU than UPF (despite lower unseen PSR), and UPF will yield higher IA than PAR (Wilcoxon signed-rank, Holm-corrected). Together these support the claim that interaction transparency and objective preference capture are dissociable properties of elicitation strategies.

**H4 (Exploratory — Strategy Ordering):** The full unseen PSR ordering UPF > PAR > DQ will be observed, with the PAR–DQ gap significantly positive after Holm correction.

**Reporting convention:** All p-values reported without leading zero (e.g., p = .042). Effect sizes reported as r for Wilcoxon tests and η² for Friedman tests.
